# Principles of Game Design

## The Game Design Process
* Fun is different per player

### What does a game designer do?
* Imagine
    * A new world, mechanics, systems
* Define
    * Create detailed definitions
* Describe
* Transmit
* Implement 

### Imagining a game
* There are no new ideas, just different arrangements of interesting things
* Sources for ideas:
    * Film, TV, books
    * Life, eduaction
    * Games (traditional, board, digital, roleplaying)

#### Design/Play/Experience framework
Designer & Design --> Play --> Experience & Player --> Designer

|   | Design | Play | Experience |
| - | - | - | - |
| Storytelling | Designer's Story | Storytelling | Player's Story |
| Gameplay  | Mechanics | Dynamics | Affect |
| User Experience  | User Interface | Interactivity | Engagement |

### Role of the designer
How do you describe the elements that make up the game?
* Conceptual
    * Text based descriptions
* Functional
    * Explicit rules
    * Flowcharts
* Artistic
    * Storyboards
    * Artist renderings

Designer needs to transmit the design to the team. 

Design documentation levels:
* High Concept 
* Game Treatment 
    * More in-depth than High Concept, but not to in-depth
* Game Design Doc
    * Living document, which changes during the design/implementation proces
    * Not a deep technical doc

Game designer should be present during the implementation. Tasks include:
* Prototyping
* Level Design
* User Interface Design
* Manage/Lead Team (sometimes)

Limitations in design:
* Money (budget)
* Time (schedule)
* Technology
* Expertise (team's skills)

## Generating Game Ideas
### Have a curious mind
* Be curious
* Ask questions
* Research the world around you
* Explore new things (life experiences)
* Think of "systems" to describe things
* Think of "why" you like or dislike things
* Record your ideas
    * Journal, notebook or blog

### Brainstorming
* What does the player DO? (gameplay)
    * What are the constraints on the player?
* Where is it done? (storytelling)
* How does the game look and feel? (user experience)
* How is the game implemented? (technology)
* What sort of emotion is this game trying to evoke in the player? (experience)

### Brainstorm proces
#### Phase 1: Rapid Ideation
* How many ideas can you get in the first 15 minutes?
* Think about, storytelling, gameplay, UX, technology -> Overall Player experience
#### Phase 2: Refine Ideas
* Refine the ideas for the next 15 minutes
* Questions to answer: see **Brainstorming**

#### Phase 3: Crunch Time
* Take 5 minutes to synthesize and summarize your "best idea" into a 60 second high concept "elevator pitch"
* Focus on one game idea for the pitch
* Give it a name
* Present it to others

### Structured Brainstorming - Idea Tree Generation
* Create 3-5 idea trees
    * Each seed is based on a passion
* Create two branches off of the resulting trunks
    * the branching ideas should be somewhat related to the parent
* As the tree grows, apply certain conditions to branches
    * For example, time period or emotion
* Look at the tree
    * Check for patterns
    * Check for cool ideas
* After brainstorming, slow cook them
    * Don't decide immediately
    * let the ideas sit for a while 
    * Reconsider  them in a new light
* When necessary, reorganize ideas
    * Use DPE framework (or another)
    * Begin to challenge ideas into constraints (resources, technology, target audience)

### Structured Brainstorming - Idea Cards
* 3 colors post-it notes
    * Noun, adjective & verb
* Write a single word on each card
* Mix the cards up
* If you build a creative phrase, write it down
    * Repeat as long as necessary

## Game World Development
### The Game World
* Gameworld is in the Design part of the DPE framework
* Mandatory fun is not fun
* Lusory Attitude
    * State of mind required to enter into the play of a game (aka, the magic circle)
    * Players accept the limitations of play, because of the pleasure a game can give them
### World Building
* Worldbuilding and gameplay should go hand in hand
    * To ignore this creates ludonarrative dissonance, the strange feeling of a disconnect between mechanics and narrative
### Game World Components
* Boundaries
    * Space
    * Time
* Setting
    * Fictional/Fantasy Component
    * Entertainment Component
* Purpose of the setting
    * Defines the reason things are the way they are in the game world
    * Influences:
        * Character and Story (backstory, dialog)
        * Nature of the world (aesthetics)
        * Game mechanics
* Varying game setting can make a different game
    * Game Mechanics vs Game Setting
    * Warcraft vs Starcraft
## Storytelling and Character Design
### Storytelling in Games
* An effective story is:
    * Credible
    * Coherent
    * Dramatically meaningful
* Parts of a good story:
    * Interesting people -> Characters
    * Interesting places -> Setting
    * Doing interesting things -> Plot
### Settings and Plot in Games
* Setting defines the reason things are the way they are in the story world
    * Backstory
    * Nature of the world (aesthetics)
    * May influence game "objects" and "rules"
* The story spine structure:
    * Once upon a time...
    * Every day...
    * But one day...
    * Because of that... (repeat as necessary)
    * Until finally...
    * And ever since then...
### Characters in Games
* Character is often the most important part of a story
    * People often care most about other people

* Type of character
    * Player character (avatar/PC)
        * Non-specific/generic character (ship, car, plant)
        * Predefined character (Nathan Drake)
        * Player designed (avatar builder)
    * Non-player characters (NPCs)
        * Enemy, allies, extras, etc
        * Populates the world

* Role of a character
    * Hero or villain?
    * Classic story archetypes:
        * Hero
        * Mentor
        * Higher Self
        * Allies
        * Shape Shifter
        * Threshold Guardian
        * Trickster
        * Shadow
        * Herald
        * etc

* Character Background
    * Character should have history
    * Beliefs, quirks, relationships, motivations
    * How will the character react?
    * 10x more depth than in game
    * Documentation
        * Main = few pages
        * Minor = few paragraphs

* Naming the character
    * Interesting, memorable, rolls of the tongue
    * Fit the character
    * Marketable
    * Not overly cliché

* Look of a character
    * Concept Art
    * Basic Sketches / Illustrations
    * Model sheets
    * Storyboards

* Give personality to characters
    * Props
    * Moves
    * Dialog
    * Voice characterization

## Documenting Game Design
* AKA "Design Script", "Design Bible" or "functional specification"
* Used by entire team for development, planning and porting
* Goal is to capture "Who, what, when, where and how of gameplay"
* Does not usually include technical implementation
* Written by lead designer with input from others

* Common info
    * Title Page
    * Table of Contents
    * Design History
    * Overview
    * Game World
    * Game Mechanics
    * Game Elements
    * Game Story
    * Game Progression (levels)
    * System Menus
    * Artificial Intelligence
* Does not contain
    * Technical Details
    * Marketing
    * Management details

* Common problems
    * Not enough information
        * Wafer-thin or ... document
    * Too much information
    * The Pie-in-the-sky Document
        * Too much different things without direction
    * Fossilized document
        * Old and not up-to-date anymore

* Documentation along the way
    * To Do's
    * Doodles
    * Flowcharts
    * Story Bible
    * Script
    * Art Bible
    * Storyboards
    * Technical Design Doc
    * QA Reports
    * Schedules and Business/Marketing
* Documentation tools
    * Best tool is what works for you
        * easy to collab on
        * easy to access
        * easy to update
        * easy to learn and use
    * Web-based rather than paper based
        * Wiki or CMS
        * Google Docs
    * Project Management
        * Trello
        * Basecamp

* Postmortem
    * What went right
    * What went wrong
    * Lessons learned

* Legalitites
    * Clearly identify ownership
        * Title page and header/footer
        * (copyright) 2021 Name. All Rights Reserved.
    * Limit distribution of documentation

## Designing Gameplay
* Gameplay design can also be called System design
* MDA framework: Mechanics, Dynamics, Aesthetics
* Aesthetics:
    * In art, concerned with nature and appreciation of beauty
    * In games, concerned with nature and apprection of "fun"
    * Both cases, aesthetics are emotional and subjective
    * Aesthetics are overloaded, so affect is used

* Mechanics
    * Determine:
        * Choices a player will be able to make in a game world
        * Ramifications those choices have on the rest of the game
    * Includes
        * Game world (board)
        * Game elements (tokens/pieces)
        * Game economy (resources)
        * Rules of play (choices/challenges/goals)

* Dynamics
    * Emerge from mechanics
    * The interaction between the player and the mechanics

* Gameplay can be seen as:
    * Rules (mechanics) > Play (dynamics) > Fun (affect)
    * Mechanics are the rules and concepts that formally specify the game-as-system
    * Dynamics is the run-time behaviour of the game-as-system
    * Affect is the desirable emotional responses evoked by the game dynamics
* Gameplay example:
    * Time Limit (Mechanics) > Time Pressure (Dynamics) > Tension (Affect)

## Level Design
* Level design happens after gameplay design
* Uses all systems created
* Often where games flaws are most apparent
* Levels are used to:
    * Enhance & Structure a game
        * Gameplay
        * Storytelling
        * Progress
        * Saving
    * Technical Limitations
        * Size / Rendering / etc
* What does the 'level designer' do?
    * Understands gameplay, story & visuals
    * Applies these to create game "scenarios"
    * May be involved with aesthetics of levels
    * May do some "gameplay scripting"

* Components of level design
    * Action
        * Amount determines pacing
    * Exploration
        * What players does between "action sequences"
        * May run counter to the flow of the game
        * Balance flow of gameplay
    * Puzzle Solving
        * Advance through the level or onto the next
        * Should not be arbitrary
        * Provide info in the level to solve the puzzle
    * Storytelling
        * Translation of the story into levels
        * Architecture, characters, interactions
        * Cut-scenes and cut-sequences
    * Aesthetics
        * Looks and sounds of the level
        * Balance Act
        * Guide the player
        * Smoke and mirrors

### Tips for level designers
* Begin with conceptual work
    * Must think why level exists (how does it fit into the whole game)
    * Develop a focus for your level
    * Provide variety from other levels, without seeming like a different game
    * Diagram and Sketch
        * Grid Paper
* Architecture of Levels
    * Design for purpose
        * Single Player
        * Deathmatch Level
        * Capture the Flag
* Goals / Objectives and Progress
    * Give the player "meaningful" goals
    * Players should know what the objectives are for the level
    * Allow player to review objectives during gameplay
    * Give progress reports to player
* Structure and Progression
    * Ease player into level and build up difficulty as they progress
    * Series of ascending arcs until climax
    * Make sure there are enough challenges to occupy the player
* Flow Control
    * Goal: Try to keep player facing challenges
    * Problem: How to contain player in a given area of a level until he has accomplished what you want him to
    * Problem: How to prevent him from returning to that area after he is done
    * Players do unexpected things
    * Do not go too far
    * Squint test: The main path should be the brightest on the screen
* Degree of Difficulty
    * Scale challenge across each level and in progressive levels
    * Learning level challenges by dying should not be a requirement
    * Average vs. Expert gamers
* Other tips
    * Avoid "head fakes" > Challenges should be rewarded
    * Use AI effectively
    * Avoid player getting stuck
    * Asset sequencing
        * Don't give players something too late or early
    * Progressively reveal eye candy

## Game Balance
* Book definition
    * A balanced game is one where the main determining factor for the success of the player is the skill level of that player
    * Objective = fair, flawless, fun
* How to balance:
    * Think logically and from all angles
    * Formal static balancing (do the math)
    * Play and Tweak!!

* What needs to be in balance?
    * Player to Player (PvP)
    * Player to Gameplay (PvE)
    * Gameplay to Gameplay (mechanics)

* PvP balancing
    * Balance = Each player has no special advantage except for skill
    * How to insure balance?
        * Symmetry
            * Level, Units, and Rules
            * Ex: Chess, Most sports
        * Functional Symmetry
            * Everything has trade-offs, but are equal
            * More health, lower damage OR Lower health, more damage
        * Assymetry
            * Can create "dominant" strategies
            * Handicap
        * Negative feedback loop
            * Losers get bonus
            * Winners get less
            * Mario Kart uses this for item "randomness"
* PvE Balancing
    * Learning curve is matched by rewards to keep player playing
    * Challenges increase with time, but gradually
    * Balance is:
        * How often to reward
        * Type of reward
        * In what amount
    * As time progresses the amount of choices a player can make increase
        * Begin small to help the player learn
    * Typical play pattern
        * Present new goal
        * Gain new skill
        * Learn / Practice
        * Mastery of Goal
        * Repeat

* Mechanic Balancing
    * Features within the game mechanics are in balance with each other
    * Goal
        * Only have useful mechanics
        * Chacne doesn't trump player skill in the long run
        * Avoiding "Dominant Strategies"
        * Manage positive feedback loops
            * Negative feedback loops are good for engaging losing players
    * Transitive relationships
        * A is better than B, B is better than C, C is worst
        * Is like an upgrade path
        * Balance by:
            * Direct costs
            * Shadow Costs
            * Progression/Regression
    * Intransitive relationships
        * A is better than B, B is better than C, C is better than A
        * Rock, paperm scissors
        * Zero sum game = perfect balance
* Conclusion
    * Balanced game = a fun game
    * Iterative design is your primary balancing tool
    * Consider the relationship of units/rules when balancing (transitive/intransitive)

## Non-digital and Digital Prototyping
* Prototype is used to explore design alternatives, test theories and confirm performance
* Goal of a prototype:
    * Flush your ideas out in a tangible fashion
    * Forego art, sound and technology while you figure out the core mechanics
    * Allows you to avoid making design mistakes later in the development
    * Allows you to get the game playable ASAP
    * Gives you a vehicle for playtesting, balancing and tweaking
    * Gives you a tool to visualize the game design and communicate it to others
* Prototyping helps you move from pure ideation to actual game design
### Types of prototypes
* Visual Prototype
    * Capture visual aesthetic and simulate appearance with little to no functionality
    * Primary tool of the artist
* Proof-of-concept Prototype
    * Early prototype
    * Test some aspect of the design without focusing on the visual aesthetics or the implementation details
    * Primary tool of the game designer
* Functional Prototype (working prototype)
    * Late Prototype
    * Simulate the final design, aesthetic, and functionality together but may be reduced in size
    * May or may not be implemented on ultimate deliverable technology
    * Tool of the entire development team
### Building a prototype
* Physical prototype
    * aka paper prototype
    * Creating one:
        * Think of the goals of game experience
            * Storytelling (Player's Story)
            * Gameplay (Affective/Emotions/Fun)
        * Prototype to achieve goals
            * World, character, designer's story
            * Mechanics
    * Next steps
        * Play game and tweak
        * Discuss
            * Is this an accurate representation of the actual game in terms of the experience goals?
            * What is missing?
    * Iterate on the design as necessary 
* Software prototype
    * Prototypes designed in software
    * Use tools to help you rapidly prototype
        * PowerPoint, Twine, Level Editors, Flash, Unity, etc...
    * May resemble actual game closer than physical prototype BUT potentially more invested

## UX Design
* User experience (UX) is
    * Everything the user sees, hears, and interacts with
    * Control system
        * How do they interact with the system?
* UX flow
    * Player --Inputs--> UI --Actions--> Mechanics
    * Mechanics --Challenges--> UI --Outputs--> Player

* Accessibility in games
    * How would your game interface change for someone who was
        * Paralyzed from the waist down
        * Paralyzed from the neck down
    * Deaf
    * Blind

* Input systems
    * Hardware
        * Modes of interactions it allows
    * Interaction Model
        * Method of communication
            * Hit button / keys
            * Use stick
            * Point and click
            * Talk
        * Language of the interaction
            * How do you form commands?
* Output systems
    * Goals:
        * Communicate (feedback to user)
        * Simply and efficiently
        * Aesthetically pleasing
    * Communication Methods
        * Visuals
        * Audio
        * Tactile Feedback
    * Make things visual!
        * Games are a visual medium
        * Most people think quicker visually
        * Minimize text
    * Keep it simple
        * Less is more
    * Follow good design principles
        * Contrast - make different things different
        * Repetition - be consistant
        * Alignment - align elements
        * Proximity - put alike things together
    * Communicating Visual Design
        * Wireframes - screen design
        * Storyboards - main view
    * In Game Screens
        * Usually broken into main view on game world and HUD elements
    * Common HUD elements
        * Performance
            * Score / High score
        * Progression / Status
            * Level display
            * Mini-Map
            * Goals
            * Lives remaining/health
        * Context specific
            * Stats on selected object
            * Controls for selected object

### Player experience design activity       
List or diagram:
    * Actions player can take in the game
    * The inputs to perform the actions
    * The outputs
        * The important out-of-game screens in the game
        * The in-game display elements
        * Other methods of output
    * Create a persona of the typical player of a game
        * Gender, age, occupation
        * Create bio
            * Likes (Interest/Hobbies)
            * Dislikes
        * Give the player a name
    * "Port" a game experience to
        * A new persona
        * A new platform
    * Document the updates to the UX that have to be made

## Playtesting
* Who do you want to playtest?
    * Members of development team
    * Traditional Playtesters
    * First-impression Testers
    * Other Game Designers or Developers
    * Non-Gamers
* Questions to ask yourself before playtesting
    * How many playtesters do you want?
    * What type of feedback do you want?
        * General Impressions ("that was fun")
        * Specific feedback ("accuracy of Z is to high compared to damage of X")
### When to test?
| When         | Who              | Goal                                         |
|--------------|------------------|----------------------------------------------|
| All the Time | Development Team | Keep them enthused and find areas to improve |
|Early playtesting|People experienced in GameDev|Find problems in gameplay early|
|Playable level|First-Impression testers|Make sure GUI and controls make sense|
|Alpha Version| Traditional Playtesters| Find major problems in gameplay (and bugs in code)|
|Late in development|Non-gamers|Final balancing|
|Shipped|Players|Refine game, future development|

### How to playtest?
* How to test?
    * General game setup
        * Have game as ready as possible for test
        * Tutorials are important
        * Metrics and logging
    * Custome game setup
        * Save points (for specific scenario testing)
        * Shortcuts (ex: to jump player around)
        * Custom version (ex: test a specific feature)
* Observe Play
    * Get them started (what to tell them?)
    * Watch screen and face
    * "Think aloud" technique
    * Don't interfere    
* Gather Feedback
    * Observe
    * Tracking play data
        * Analyze (auto-) collected data for playtest
    * Post interview
    * Post survey
* Computer-based testing
    * When you need to do things over-and-over and a human is not really needed
    * Ex: level loading, repetitive actions, endurance, simulated play
* Tips for playtesting
    * You can't please everyone
    * Don't listen to idiots
    * Don't be an idiot
        * Accept suggestions that solve problems and amplify DPE goals
        * Reject suggestions that confuse the player, detract from the goal or upset the balance
    * Playtest your level
        * Should be testing internally all the time
        * Should start testing outside the team ASAP
